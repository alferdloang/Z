Verb Tenses & Aspect

The English verb system comprises twelve core tense-aspect combinations, organised across three temporal frames (past, present, future) and four aspectual categories (simple, continuous, perfect, perfect continuous).

Present Tenses

  • Simple Present: Habitual actions & general truths.
  • Present Continuous: Ongoing actions at the moment of speech.
  • Present Perfect: Past actions with present relevance.
  • Present Perfect Continuous: Duration from past to present.

Past Tenses

  • Simple Past: Completed actions at a specific past time.
  • Past Continuous: Ongoing actions interrupted by another event.
  • Past Perfect: Actions completed before another past moment.
● Source Reference
Quirk, R., Greenbaum, S., Leech, G., & Svartvik, J. (1985). A Comprehensive Grammar of the English Language. Longman.

Parts of Speech & Syntax

English syntax follows a predominantly Subject-Verb-Object (SVO) word order. The eight traditional parts of speech form the building blocks of every grammatical construction in the language.

The Eight Categories

  1. Nouns -- entities, concepts, people, places.
  2. Verbs -- actions, states, occurrences.
  3. Adjectives -- modifiers describing nouns.
  4. Adverbs -- modifiers for verbs, adjectives, or clauses.
  5. Pronouns -- substitutes for nouns.
  6. Prepositions -- relational words indicating position/time.
  7. Conjunctions -- connectors between clauses or phrases.
  8. Interjections -- exclamatory expressions.
● Source Reference
Huddleston, R. & Pullum, G. K. (2002). The Cambridge Grammar of the English Language. Cambridge University Press.

Ref. 001-A Ephemeral

Lasting for a very short time; transitory by nature.

"The ephemeral beauty of cherry blossoms reminds us to cherish each moment."

Adjective
Ref. 002-B Sycophant

A person who acts obsequiously toward someone to gain advantage.

"The court was filled with sycophants who flattered the monarch relentlessly."

Noun
Ref. 003-C Pragmatic

Dealing with things sensibly and realistically; practical.

"Her pragmatic approach to budgeting saved the company from collapse."

Adjective
Ref. 101-ID Bury the Hatchet

To end a quarrel or conflict and make peace.

"After years of rivalry, the two CEOs decided to bury the hatchet and collaborate."

Idiom
Ref. 102-ID Turn a Blind Eye

To deliberately ignore something that one knows is wrong.

"The manager turned a blind eye to the minor infractions to maintain morale."

Idiom
Ref. 004-D Laconic

Using very few words to express meaning; concise to the point of terseness.

"His laconic reply -- 'Fine.' -- spoke volumes about his disappointment."
